\begin{abstract}
Dynamic inference can reduce average compute by using a small model for easy
inputs and deferring harder inputs to a larger model. This work studies whether
the two models in such a big/little cascade should be searched independently or
jointly as one cascade. We propose a constrained NSGA-II co-search pipeline for
CIFAR-10 that searches little-big CNN pairs under MCU-motivated memory and
parameter constraints, optimizing proxy cascade accuracy and expected cascade
FLOPs. The joint search is compared with an independent NAS baseline where
little and big models are searched separately and combined afterwards. We also
evaluate four retraining objectives: cross-entropy, cross-entropy with label
smoothing, a cascade-aware co-training loss, and the same cascade-aware loss
with label smoothing. Under a practical compute budget with 5-epoch proxy
training and 50-epoch final retraining, joint co-search finds stronger final
operating points than the independent baseline at the fixed confidence
threshold $\tau=0.70$. The cheaper cascade-aware variant reaches 79.69\%
accuracy at 1.67M MACs, while the highest-accuracy joint variant reaches
80.69\% at 2.08M MACs; the independent baseline reaches 74.03\% at 2.22M MACs.
A Raspberry Pi Pico 2 deployment further shows lower measured latency and
route-aware energy for the joint cascade than for the independent baseline.
The results show that, within this constrained training setting, the
accuracy-FLOPs trade-off is shaped not only by model accuracy, but also by
threshold choice, exit ratio, routing error, and capacity allocation between
the little and big models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Deep neural networks have become increasingly accurate, but their computational
cost makes deployment difficult on resource-constrained devices. Edge and
embedded systems often have strict limits on memory, latency, and energy, while
still needing reliable predictions. A common way to reduce inference cost is
dynamic inference: instead of spending the same computation on every input, the
system adapts its computation to the difficulty of each sample. In a
big/little cascade, a small model is evaluated first. If the small model is
confident, the system exits early; otherwise, the sample is deferred to a larger
model. Cascades can decide this route in different ways, for example using a
learned gate, a margin-based rule, or a confidence threshold. This report uses a
maximum-softmax confidence threshold because it adds no extra routing model, has
negligible overhead, and is easy to evaluate under embedded constraints. Such a
cascade can reduce average FLOPs substantially, but only if the little model is
accurate enough on easy samples, the big model is strong enough on deferred
samples, and the threshold routes examples correctly.

Designing such cascades is therefore not only a problem of finding two accurate
models. The two models must work together under a shared routing rule and a
shared deployment budget. If the little model is too small, it may rarely exit
correctly. If the big model receives too much capacity, the cascade may become
accurate but expensive. If the confidence threshold is too low, confident
wrong exits can damage accuracy; if it is too high, most samples defer and the
cascade loses its computational benefit. These trade-offs make cascade design a
multi-objective problem involving accuracy, FLOPs, exit ratio, memory, and
calibration.

Neural architecture search (NAS) is a natural tool for this setting because it
can explore many architectural design choices under explicit constraints
\cite{elsken2019nassurvey}. Prior work has studied NAS for efficient models,
multi-objective architecture search, and dynamic inference, including early-exit
networks and model cascades \cite{deb2002nsga2,lu2019nsganet,chebykin2022encas}.
However, many cascade systems either design models independently or focus on
placing exits inside a single backbone (Table~\ref{tab:related-comparison}).
This leaves open the question of whether
a little and big model should be searched jointly as a pair, so that the search
objective directly reflects cascade behavior.

This report studies that question using a CIFAR-10 big/little cascade under an
edge-style deployment constraint. We compare a joint co-search method, where
NSGA-II searches complete little-big pairs, with an independent baseline, where
little and big models are searched separately and combined afterwards. Both
methods use the same underlying CNN search space, the same proxy-training
budget, and comparable resource constraints. The joint search optimizes cascade
accuracy and average cascade FLOPs directly, while the independent baseline
tests whether good standalone models are sufficient to produce good cascades.

Architecture search is only part of the problem. Since cascade routing depends
on confidence, the final training objective may also matter. Cross-entropy (CE)
optimizes classification accuracy, but it does not explicitly account for the
fact that some samples should exit at the little model while others should
defer. Confidence calibration and label smoothing are also relevant because
miscalibrated confidence can cause incorrect early exits
\cite{guo2017calibration,mueller2019labelsmoothing,li2021learningtocascade}.
We therefore evaluate the selected joint architectures under four retraining
settings: CE, CE with label smoothing, a cascade-aware loss, and the
cascade-aware loss with label smoothing. This separates the architecture-search
question from the training-objective question.

The report is organized around three research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Does joint NAS co-search of a little/big model pair
    produce a better final cascade accuracy versus average cascade FLOPs
    trade-off than independently searched and combined model pairs under matched
    search spaces and edge-memory constraints?

    \item \textbf{RQ2:} After architectures are selected by joint NAS, does
    cascade-aware co-training improve cascade accuracy, routing quality, or
    calibration compared with standard CE and CE with label smoothing?

    \item \textbf{RQ3:} How do threshold choice, exit ratio, and model-size
    allocation explain the observed accuracy/FLOPs trade-offs?
\end{itemize}

Taken together, these questions define the experimental framework studied in
this report: constrained joint architecture search for NAS-driven big/little
dynamic inference, a matched independent-search baseline, cascade-aware
retraining, and threshold-sweep evaluation of input-adaptive routing. In this
work, framework refers to the complete experimental pipeline for constrained
joint cascade search, retraining, and threshold-based evaluation, rather than a
general-purpose reusable software package. The implementation and experiment
scripts are available in the project repository.\footnote{\url{LINK}}
The final evaluation shows that the framework's main advantage comes from
designing the cascade as a coupled system: the joint variants improve the
accuracy-FLOPs trade-off over the independently searched baseline at the fixed
operating threshold. The cascade-aware loss is therefore treated as a
retraining ablation rather than the sole contribution; it is useful for
analyzing and tuning routing behaviour, while the C1-C4 variants expose the
trade-off between cheaper early exits and higher-accuracy deferral.

The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a joint NSGA-II co-search setup for big/little CNN cascades under memory and parameter constraints;
    \item a cascade-aware co-training loss using soft routing probabilities to align training with threshold-based inference;
    \item an ablation study of CE, CE with label smoothing, cascade-aware loss, and cascade-aware loss with label smoothing; and
    \item an analysis showing that joint cascade design improves the observed accuracy/FLOPs trade-off, while threshold, exit ratio, routing errors, and capacity allocation explain the remaining differences between variants; and
    \item a Raspberry Pi Pico 2 deployment evaluation reporting on-device latency, route split, deployment-memory observations, and route-aware energy consumption.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the report is structured as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews dynamic inference, NAS for efficient models,
and confidence calibration for cascade routing. Section~\ref{sec:methodology}
describes the cascade model, search space, joint and independent NAS procedures,
and retraining losses. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents the experimental
setup and evaluation metrics. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the results for
the three research questions. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the main
findings, limitations, and future work, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
concludes the report.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Dynamic Inference and Big/Little Cascading}
\label{sec:related:dynamic}

Dynamic neural networks adapt their computation to each input instead of using a
fixed inference path for all samples. Han et al.\ survey this area and group
dynamic models into instance-wise, spatial-wise, and temporal-wise adaptation
methods \cite{han2021dynamic}. Big/little cascades are an instance-wise dynamic
inference method: an inexpensive model handles easy samples, while difficult
samples are routed to a more expensive model. Park et al.\ proposed an early
big/little DNN system for low-power inference, showing that a small network can
filter easy inputs before invoking a larger network \cite{park2015biglittle}.
This idea is attractive for embedded systems because the expected cost depends
on the exit ratio, not only on the worst-case cost of the largest model.

Closely related work studies early-exit networks, where auxiliary classifiers
are attached to intermediate layers of a single backbone. BranchyNet introduced
side branches that allow confident samples to exit before reaching the final
layer \cite{teerapittayanon2017branchynet}. MSDNet extends this idea with
multi-scale feature representations for anytime and budgeted classification
\cite{huang2018msdnet}. Shallow-Deep Networks analyze ``overthinking'', where a
deep network can change an already-correct intermediate prediction into an
incorrect final one, and use early exits to reduce unnecessary computation
\cite{kaya2019shallowdeep}. These methods demonstrate the value of adaptive
computation, but they primarily modify a single backbone. Our work instead
studies a cascade of two independent CNNs and asks how capacity should be
allocated between the little and big models.

The routing rule is central to both early-exit networks and model cascades.
Most systems use confidence thresholds: a sample exits if the intermediate or
little model is sufficiently confident. This makes calibration important,
because overconfident wrong predictions directly become wrong early exits.
Learning to Cascade addresses this issue by calibrating confidence specifically
for cascade systems, showing that standard calibration methods do not always
optimize the accuracy-cost trade-off of a cascade \cite{li2021learningtocascade}.
This motivates our separate analysis of confidence, routing errors, and
retraining losses.

% Related-work subsection divider
\subsection{NAS for Dynamic Inference}
\label{sec:related:nasdynamic}

NAS automates architecture design by defining a search space, a search
strategy, and a performance-estimation method \cite{elsken2019nassurvey}. It is
particularly relevant for dynamic inference because accuracy and compute must be
optimized jointly. Multi-objective evolutionary NAS is a common fit for this
setting, since it can produce a Pareto front rather than a single scalarized
solution. NSGA-II is a standard elitist multi-objective genetic algorithm
\cite{deb2002nsga2}, and NSGA-Net applies multi-objective evolutionary search
to neural architectures \cite{lu2019nsganet}. We follow this general
multi-objective view, but apply it to cascade pairs under explicit memory and
parameter constraints.

Several recent methods apply NAS to early-exit neural networks. EDANAS searches
early-exit networks by adapting an Once-for-All style search space and jointly
considering accuracy and MACs \cite{gambella2023edanas}. NACHOS extends this
line toward hardware-constrained early-exit design, searching for networks that
satisfy accuracy and MAC constraints at inference time
\cite{gambella2025nachos}. These approaches are close in spirit because they
use architecture search for adaptive inference. However, they focus on adding
or optimizing exits within a single network. Our method searches two separate
role-specific networks and optimizes their behavior as a cascade pair.

Other work focuses on learning the exit decision itself. JEI-DNN jointly learns
the gating mechanism and intermediate inference modules, addressing the
train-test mismatch that arises when confidence-threshold gates are trained
separately from the prediction modules \cite{regol2024jeidnn}. This line of
work is complementary to ours: JEI-DNN improves learned gating inside dynamic
neural networks, while our study keeps a simple threshold router and asks
whether the architectures of the two cascade members should be searched jointly.

ENCAS is the closest related work on NAS for model cascades. It performs
evolutionary neural cascade search across pretrained supernetworks to obtain a
front of cascades trading accuracy against FLOPs \cite{chebykin2022encas}. Our
work differs in two ways. First, we co-search the little and big models from a
small role-specific CNN search space under a strict edge-memory budget, rather
than composing subnetworks from pretrained supernetworks. Second, we explicitly
compare joint co-search against an independently searched baseline and then
study whether cascade-aware retraining improves the selected architectures.

% Related-work subsection divider
\subsection{Confidence Calibration for Cascade Routing}
\label{sec:related:calibration}

Modern neural networks are often miscalibrated: their predicted probabilities
can be more confident than their empirical accuracy \cite{guo2017calibration}.
This is especially important for cascades because the confidence score is not
only a diagnostic quantity; it controls computation. A poorly calibrated little
model can exit on samples that should have been deferred to the big model. As a
result, optimizing standalone classification accuracy is not necessarily enough
to optimize cascade accuracy.

Label smoothing is one common training modification that can affect both
accuracy and calibration. Muller et al.\ show that label smoothing changes the
structure of learned representations and can improve generalization, although
its effect depends on the task and downstream use \cite{mueller2019labelsmoothing}.
Focal loss has also been studied as a calibration-aware alternative; Mukhoti et
al.\ show that it can improve calibration in some settings
\cite{mukhoti2020calibrating}. These results motivate treating the final
training objective as part of the cascade design, rather than evaluating only
the searched architecture.

Cascade-specific calibration is more directly aligned with our problem.
Learning to Cascade argues that calibration should be optimized for the
accuracy-cost behavior of the whole cascade, not only for matching confidence
to model accuracy \cite{li2021learningtocascade}. Our cascade-aware loss follows
the same motivation but is used as a retraining objective for a jointly searched
big/little pair. It introduces soft routing weights during training so that
little-model and big-model losses are weighted according to the expected route
of each sample. The goal is to improve cascade behavior without replacing the
simple threshold rule used at inference.


% Related-work subsection divider
\subsection{Resource-Constrained NAS}
\label{sec:related:hwnas}

Hardware-aware NAS extends architecture search with deployment constraints such
as latency, memory, energy, FLOPs, or parameter count. This is important because
the most accurate architecture under an unconstrained search is often not
deployable on edge hardware. Surveys of hardware-aware NAS describe how search
objectives can be coupled to device-specific measurements or analytical proxies
\cite{benmeziane2021hwnas}. NetAdapt iteratively adapts a pretrained network to
meet platform constraints \cite{yang2018netadapt}, while ProxylessNAS searches
directly on the target task and hardware instead of relying only on proxy tasks
\cite{cai2019proxylessnas}. MobileNetV3 combines platform-aware search with
efficient mobile building blocks \cite{howard2019mobilenetv3}.

During NAS, our search uses analytical resource constraints rather than measured
latency: deployment memory, parameter count, and average cascade FLOPs. This
keeps architecture selection reproducible and independent of measurement noise
from a specific hardware run. Measured Pico 2 latency is treated separately as a
deployment evaluation after model selection. The memory constraint is applied to
the whole cascade, not just to each model separately. This matters because a
good cascade is a capacity-allocation problem: the little model must be large
enough to exit correctly, while the big model must still have enough capacity to
improve deferred samples.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section describes the cascade inference system, the architecture search
space, and the two design procedures compared in the experiments. The
methodology separates two questions: RQ1 evaluates whether the two models
should be searched jointly or independently, while RQ2 evaluates whether the
final retraining loss improves the selected cascades.

\subsection{Cascade Architecture and Routing}

The cascade consists of two independent CNNs: a lightweight little model and a larger big model. For each input $x$, the little model is evaluated first. Its confidence is computed as the maximum softmax probability,
\[
c_l = \max(\mathrm{softmax}(z_l)),
\]
where $z_l$ are the little-model logits. If $c_l > \tau$, the cascade accepts the little model's prediction and exits early. Otherwise, the input is deferred to the big model.

Figure~\ref{fig:cascade-routing} shows this threshold-based routing rule. Every input is processed by the little model, but only inputs below the confidence threshold $\tau$ are sent to the big model.

We report the exit ratio $\rho$, defined as the fraction of samples handled by the little model alone. The expected cascade FLOPs are then
\[
F_{\mathrm{cascade}} = F_l + (1-\rho)F_b,
\]
where $F_l$ and $F_b$ are the FLOPs of the little and big models. This metric captures the dynamic-inference cost: every sample pays for the little model, while only deferred samples pay for the big model. We also report routing error rate, which measures cases where the little model exits incorrectly although the big model would have predicted correctly.

\subsection{Search Space and Resource Constraints}

Both models are sampled from the same CNN search space, but with role-specific channel ranges and depths. The little model uses the smaller role configuration, while the big model uses the larger one. Table~\ref{tab:search-space} summarizes the searchable block counts, channel choices, and per-block operations used by both the joint and independent NAS experiments.

The search space is intentionally small and discrete. This keeps evolutionary
search feasible under the available compute budget while still allowing
meaningful variation in depth, width, convolution type, and residual
connections. The little and big channel ranges are separated so that the little
model is biased toward cheaper architectures, while the big model has enough
capacity to serve as a fallback for harder samples. The block ranges follow the
same role separation: the little model is limited to fewer stages, while the big
model can use deeper feature extraction.

All candidates must satisfy deployment constraints motivated by embedded
inference. The deployment-memory estimate includes both models' weights, the
input buffer, and the larger of the two peak activation footprints, since the
cascade evaluates the models sequentially. Joint NAS enforces a
450\,KiB deployment-memory cap, a 50K-100K combined-parameter
window, role-level lower bounds of little $\geq$15K and big $\geq$40K
parameters, role-level upper bounds of little $\leq$30K and big $\leq$85K
parameters, the role-order constraint that the big model must have at least as
many parameters as the little model, and the structural rule that the big model
must have more blocks than the little model. The independent baseline uses the
same search space and role-order rules, but searches the roles separately with
the same explicit parameter ranges: 15K-30K for little and 40K-85K for big.

The 450\,KiB cap is chosen as a practical target for deployment on a 512\,KiB
MCU. It leaves some margin for runtime overhead, stack usage, framework code,
and other buffers instead of assuming that the full 512\,KiB can be used only
for model storage and activations. This creates a tight embedded-memory setting
where the search must make meaningful capacity trade-offs instead of simply
increasing both models. The parameter lower bounds prevent the search from
exploiting extremely small models that are cheap but not useful classifiers,
while the upper bounds keep the two roles within the same capacity envelope for
both search strategies. The big-model upper bound is higher because a 15K little
model can still be paired with an 85K fallback without exceeding the 100K
combined cap. The role-order constraints prevent the first-stage model from
becoming larger than the fallback model. Together, the parameter and block-order
rules enforce a clear separation between the cheap first-stage model and the
more expressive fallback model.

The memory and FLOP constraints used during search are analytical estimates.
Parameters and activations are counted as float32 values, not quantized weights.
This keeps the search reproducible across execution environments, but it also
means the NAS resource numbers should be interpreted as model-level deployment
proxies. Direct Pico 2 latency is reported separately after the architectures
have been selected and exported.

\subsection{Joint Co-Search with NSGA-II}

The joint NAS experiment searches little-big model pairs directly. Each
candidate encodes both architectures and is first checked against the hard
memory, parameter, and depth-order constraints. Infeasible candidates are
rejected analytically and are not proxy-trained. Feasible candidates are
proxy-trained for 5 epochs using the cascade-aware shared loss, then evaluated
as a cascade at a fixed threshold $\tau=0.70$. The co-search proxy loss uses
$k=20$, $\lambda_l=0.75$, $\lambda_b=0.05$, $\lambda_d=0.01$, and
$\lambda_w=0.075$, with no label smoothing. The 5-epoch proxy budget is a
compromise: it is long enough to separate clearly weak architectures from
promising ones, but short enough to evaluate hundreds of candidates within the
available compute budget.

The threshold is fixed during architecture search rather than optimized as part
of the genotype. This avoids a shortcut where the search can improve proxy
cascade accuracy by choosing a high threshold that defers almost every sample to
the big model. Thresholds are instead swept after final retraining, where the
accuracy-FLOPs trade-off can be analyzed explicitly. The value $\tau=0.70$ was
chosen as a practical proxy-search setting: it is low enough that the little
model can exit on some samples during proxy evaluation, but high enough that
early exits still require meaningful confidence.

Infeasible architectures are filtered before proxy training using analytical
parameter, memory, and block-order checks. The evolutionary initialization and
offspring repair steps also attempt to sample or repair candidates into the
feasible region. This prevents the search budget from being dominated by invalid
architectures and makes the number of proxy-trained candidates more meaningful.
For joint co-search, these checks include both pair-level constraints and
role-level little/big parameter ranges before the candidate is proxy-trained.

NSGA-II is used because the search is naturally multi-objective: we want high cascade accuracy and low average cascade FLOPs under hard deployment constraints. Rather than choosing an arbitrary scalar weighting between accuracy and compute, NSGA-II produces a Pareto front of architecture pairs and makes the accuracy-compute trade-off explicit. It is also a practical choice for this study because it is well-established, straightforward to implement for mixed discrete architecture variables, and sufficient for answering whether joint pair search improves the observed accuracy-FLOPs trade-off. The two objectives are therefore to maximize proxy cascade accuracy and minimize expected cascade FLOPs. Candidates with exit ratios outside the desired routing window are softly penalized, so the search does not only find accurate pairs that almost always defer to the big model.

After the search, three candidates are selected for full retraining from the
proxy Pareto region. The selector keeps only candidates whose expected cascade
FLOPs are lower than the big model's standalone FLOPs,
$F_{\mathrm{cascade}} < F_b$, so the selected pair represents a genuine
compute-saving cascade rather than a slower two-stage system. Within this
filtered region, the chosen candidates are the knee point of the proxy
accuracy-FLOPs frontier and its nearest unique neighbours. This favors
architectures around the point where additional compute gives diminishing
proxy-accuracy gains, while avoiding duplicate genotypes. The selection rule
therefore does not simply take the three highest proxy-accuracy candidates;
it chooses representative candidates from the compute-saving knee region and
leaves final performance claims to the fully retrained test results. The proxy
frontier and selected candidates are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:appendix-proxy-selection} in
Appendix~\ref{app:diagnostic-plots}.

\subsection{Independent NAS Baseline}

The independent baseline is designed to test whether joint pair search provides
value beyond searching two good models separately. In this baseline, little and
big architectures are optimized in two separate NSGA-II searches. Each search
optimizes standalone validation accuracy against parameter count within its
role-specific budget. Parameter count is used at this stage because each model
is searched before cascade routing exists, so expected cascade FLOPs cannot yet
be computed. Generated role candidates are checked analytically against their
role-specific parameter ranges before proxy training: little candidates must be
within 15K-30K parameters, while big candidates must be within 40K-85K
parameters. The initial population samples only role-feasible candidates, and
offspring outside the configured role range are repaired by resampling a
feasible replacement before evaluation. The resulting role-specific Pareto
candidates are then combined post hoc into cascade pairs. At this stage, the
combiner enumerates all little-Pareto by big-Pareto pairings rather than taking
only the top few models from each role. Pair-level feasibility is checked after
combination, including the total parameter budget, deployment-memory cap, and
the role-order rules that the big model must have at least as many parameters
and more blocks than the little model. The final independent cascades are ranked
and selected from these feasible pairs. This means that a high-accuracy 85K big
model is not discarded simply because it cannot pair with a 30K little model; it
can still be selected if it forms a valid cascade with a smaller little model.

The baseline is matched to the joint search where this is meaningful: it uses
the same CNN search space, seed, proxy-training duration, threshold sweep, and
deployment constraints. It is intentionally not identical in objective, because
its purpose is to test whether standalone little and big models are sufficient,
or whether searching the two roles together under a cascade objective adds
value. The combined independent pairs are retrained and evaluated using the
same threshold sweep as the joint candidates.

Figure~\ref{fig:search-pipeline} summarizes the two design pipelines. The joint
pipeline evaluates little-big pairs during search, while the independent
baseline delays cascade construction until after the separate role-specific
searches have finished.

\subsection{Cascade-Aware Loss}

We introduce a cascade-aware retraining loss that keeps standard classification terms, but also reflects how the cascade is used at inference time. In this work, \emph{co-training} means jointly optimizing the little and big models in the same cascade pair with a shared loss. It does not refer to semi-supervised co-training with separate data views. The key idea is to replace the hard routing decision in Figure~\ref{fig:cascade-routing} with a differentiable approximation during training. Figure~\ref{fig:cotraining-loss-flow} shows how the loss is constructed from the two model outputs and the soft routing probability.

First, we compute the little model's confidence:
\[
c_l = \max(\mathrm{softmax}(z_l)),
\]
where $z_l$ are the little-model logits. Instead of directly applying the hard rule $c_l > \tau$, we define a soft exit probability:
\[
p_{\mathrm{exit}} = \sigma(k(c_l-\tau)),
\]
where $\tau$ is the routing threshold, $k$ controls how sharp the soft decision is, and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. If the little model is confident, $p_{\mathrm{exit}}$ becomes close to 1. If it is uncertain, $p_{\mathrm{exit}}$ becomes close to 0.

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:cotraining-loss-flow}, a high-confidence
little prediction is treated as a likely early exit, so the little-model CE term
is emphasized. A low-confidence little prediction is treated as a likely
deferral, so the big-model CE term is emphasized. The routing terms are small
regularizers rather than the main training signal: they encourage the cascade
to avoid deferring every sample, while also discouraging confident wrong
little-model exits.

The full cascade-aware loss is:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cascade}}
={}& (p_{\mathrm{exit}}+\lambda_l)\mathrm{CE}(z_l,y) \\
&+ (1-p_{\mathrm{exit}}+\lambda_b)\mathrm{CE}(z_b,y) \\
&+ \lambda_d(1-p_{\mathrm{exit}}) \\
&+ \lambda_w p_{\mathrm{exit}}
   \mathbf{1}[\hat{y}_l \neq y].
\end{aligned}
\]

The first term trains the little model. It is weighted by the soft exit probability because the little model is responsible for samples likely to exit early. The auxiliary weight $\lambda_l$ ensures that the little model still receives a stable classification signal even for samples that are likely to be deferred.

The second term trains the big model. It is weighted by $1-p_{\mathrm{exit}}$, so the big model is trained more strongly on samples that the little model is likely to defer. The optional auxiliary weight $\lambda_b$ keeps a small constant big-model training signal even for samples that are likely to exit early.

The third term, $\lambda_d(1-p_{\mathrm{exit}})$, is a small defer cost. It discourages the system from sending every sample to the big model, which would increase accuracy but destroy the FLOPs benefit of the cascade. This weight is kept small because the model should not be forced to exit when the little model is genuinely uncertain; if the penalty is too large, it can dominate the classification objective and encourage harmful early exits.

The final term, $\lambda_w p_{\mathrm{exit}}\mathbf{1}[\hat{y}_l \neq y]$, penalizes confident wrong exits. This is the most cascade-specific part: it targets the harmful case where the little model would accept a sample even though its prediction is wrong. Like the defer cost, it is used as a regularizer so that the classification losses remain the dominant optimization signal; overly large values could reduce branch accuracy or calibration instead of improving cascade behavior.

Overall, the loss tries to balance three goals: keep the little model accurate, keep the big model useful for deferred samples, and encourage early exits only when they are likely to be correct. In the final C3 and C4 experiments we use routing sharpness $k=20$, $\lambda_l=0.75$, $\lambda_b=0.05$, $\lambda_d=0.0125$, and $\lambda_w=0.075$. C3 uses no label smoothing, while C4 uses label smoothing of 0.1.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}

All experiments use CIFAR-10 \cite{krizhevsky2009cifar}. We use the official
CIFAR-10 training split for training and validation, with a deterministic 45K/5K
split: the first 5K images are used for validation and the remaining 45K images
are used for training. The official 10K CIFAR-10 test split is kept separate and
is used only for final threshold-sweep evaluation after retraining. CIFAR-10 is
small enough to support repeated NAS proxy training and ablation runs, while
still being non-trivial for confidence-based routing and calibration analysis.

The training split uses random crop with padding 4, random horizontal flipping,
conversion to tensor, and CIFAR-10 normalization. The validation and test splits
use only tensor conversion and the same normalization. The validation split is
used during proxy NAS evaluation and joint-retraining checkpoint selection. The
test split is not used for architecture selection.

\subsection{Baselines}

RQ1 compares two architecture-design procedures. The first is joint co-search,
where NSGA-II searches complete little-big cascade pairs and evaluates them
directly as cascades. The second is an independent NAS baseline, where the
little and big architectures are searched separately and then combined after the
two searches. Both approaches use the CNN search space in
Table~\ref{tab:search-space}, the same random seed, the same proxy-training
duration, and the same final threshold sweep.

This comparison should be interpreted as an end-to-end cascade-design
comparison rather than a perfectly isolated test of architecture search alone.
The joint pipeline searches complete cascade pairs and uses cascade-aware pair
training during proxy evaluation, while the independent baseline searches the
two roles as standalone classifiers and trains the combined models
independently with CE. Therefore, any RQ1 improvement reflects the combined
effect of joint pair selection, cascade-aware proxy training, and matched
resource constraints.

The joint search uses a population size of 20 for 20 generations. The
independent baseline uses a population size of 20 for 10 generations for each
role-specific search. Since it runs one little-model search and one big-model
search, this gives the independent baseline approximately the same number of
proxy-trained candidates as the joint search. This matches the
proxy-evaluation budget more closely than the evolutionary depth per role:
joint NAS evolves complete little-big pairs for 20 generations, while each
independent role is evolved for 10 generations because the two searches are run
sequentially and were substantially more time-consuming in practice. During
search, joint candidates are evaluated at the fixed routing threshold
$\tau=0.70$. Independent candidates are searched as standalone classifiers using
CE and optimized for validation accuracy against parameter count. Parameter
count is used here because the models are searched before a cascade route
exists, so expected cascade FLOPs are not yet defined. After the two
role-specific searches finish, the independent baseline combines the resulting
Pareto fronts by enumerating all little-big pairs, rejecting pairs that violate
the combined parameter budget, deployment-memory budget, or role-order rules,
and selecting the highest-scored feasible cascade pairs. The selected little
and big architectures are then trained as separate CE classifiers for 50
epochs; no cascade-aware shared loss is applied to the independent baseline.
They are only coupled during final evaluation, where the little model's
confidence threshold determines whether an input exits early or defers to the
big model. After combination, the independent cascades are evaluated with the
same threshold sweep and cascade-FLOPs metric as the joint method. Some RQ1
plots use the C4 joint retrain directory to show the high-accuracy end of the
joint ablation against the independently searched cascades, while the separate
C1-C4 comparison in RQ2 reports how the final retraining loss changes
accuracy, routing, and expected FLOPs for the same selected joint architectures.

For RQ2, the selected joint-NAS architectures are retrained under the four loss
conditions in Table~\ref{tab:retraining-loss-ablations}. Each condition retrains
the same three selected architectures, so the ablation compares retraining
objectives rather than different architecture selections. Retraining starts
from scratch rather than continuing from proxy-trained weights. This separates
the short proxy phase, which is used only to choose architectures, from the
longer final training phase used for reported results. The 50-epoch retraining
budget is long enough to obtain reasonably stable comparisons, with smaller
improvements expected in later epochs; a longer schedule such as 100 epochs
would likely be more precise, but was not used because of the total compute cost
of retraining multiple architectures under multiple loss settings.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary metrics are cascade accuracy and expected cascade FLOPs. Cascade
accuracy is the accuracy after applying the threshold router: samples above the
threshold use the little prediction, while deferred samples use the big
prediction. Expected cascade FLOPs are computed analytically as
$F_l+(1-\rho)F_b$, where $\rho$ is the measured exit ratio.

We also report exit ratio, little-model accuracy, big-model accuracy, parameter
count, and estimated deployment memory. Deployment memory is estimated in
float32 and includes both models' weights, the input buffer, and the larger peak
activation footprint of the two sequential paths. FLOPs are reported as MACs
and are estimated analytically from the selected architectures. These analytical
metrics are used for NAS selection and the main accuracy-compute comparisons;
measured Pico 2 latency is reported separately in the deployment evaluation.

For routing and calibration analysis, we report little-model ECE and routing
error rate. ECE is computed with 15 confidence bins using the little model's
maximum softmax confidence. Routing error rate is the fraction of test samples
where the cascade exits at the little model, the little prediction is wrong, and
the big model would have been correct. This captures the specific failure mode
caused by a confident wrong early exit.

Proxy-search results and final retrained results are treated separately. A
proxy result is a validation metric measured after short 5-epoch training and is
used only for architecture selection. Proxy Pareto fronts therefore summarize
which candidates looked promising under the cheap search budget. Final figures
and comparisons use retrained checkpoints and test-set threshold sweeps, so
final claims are not based on the proxy-search validation scores.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and executed through a scripted
pipeline that runs joint NAS, independent NAS, retraining, metric aggregation,
and plot generation. CUDA is used when available, and CPU execution is used
otherwise.

All CIFAR-10 experiments use seed 42. The implementation seeds Python, NumPy,
PyTorch, CUDA when available, NSGA-II, feasible sampling and repair, and
DataLoader shuffling. Search candidates are proxy-trained for 5 epochs, while
final selected architectures are retrained from scratch for 50 epochs.

Experiment outputs are stored as CSV files, including search logs, Pareto
fronts, selected architectures, training curves, and per-threshold test sweeps.
All plots and analysis are generated from these saved outputs, so the reported
figures can be reproduced without re-running model training.

\subsection{Pico 2 Deployment Test Setup}

After final retraining, the selected cascades were exported to ONNX and then to
float32 TensorFlow Lite models for TensorFlow Lite Micro. The exported little
and big models were converted into C arrays and compiled into Raspberry Pi Pico
2 firmware. The Pico firmware implements the same threshold router used in the
offline evaluation: each image is first evaluated by the little model, the
maximum softmax confidence is compared with $\tau=0.70$, and only
low-confidence samples are deferred to the big model.

Two Pico experiments were run. First, the normal latency and accuracy test used
a USB serial protocol in which the host streamed CIFAR-10 test images to the
Pico one sample at a time. This allowed the full 10,000-image CIFAR-10 test set
to be evaluated without storing the entire dataset in Pico flash. For each
sample, the Pico reported the predicted class, route decision, correctness,
little-model time, big-model time when used, total cascade time, and analytical
FLOP counts for the selected path. This is the source of the deployment latency
and route-split results reported in Table~\ref{tab:pico-deployment}.

Second, a route-aware energy test was run on a 1,000-image embedded CIFAR-10
subset. This version stores the test subset in Pico flash and runs without USB
power during measurement. Figure~\ref{fig:pico-energy-setup} shows the
measurement circuit. An Elegoo Uno R3 powers the Pico 2 through a 10~$\Omega$
high-side shunt connected to VSYS. The Uno samples the voltage before and after
the shunt to estimate current and power, while Pico GP18 drives an inference
trigger connected to Uno D2. In the route-aware build, Pico GP19 also indicates
the route decision to Uno D3, with the high state representing deferred
big-model execution. This setup separates idle time from the active inference
window and records whether each measured inference used the little path or the
deferred path.

\subsection{RQ3: Threshold, Exit Ratio, and Routing}

The threshold-based cascade does not depend only on the standalone accuracy of
the little and big models. It also depends on how confidence separates safe
little-model exits from samples that should defer to the big model. This is
visible in the routing metrics. At $\tau=0.70$, C4 has a routing error rate of
0.0083, while C1 and C3 have routing error rates of 0.0231 and 0.0242,
respectively. Thus, the highest-accuracy variants also make fewer harmful early
exit decisions.

However, ECE and routing error do not move in the same direction. C1 and C3
have lower little-model ECE than C2 and C4, but they also have higher routing
error. This means that global calibration, as measured by ECE, is not
sufficient to explain cascade quality. What matters for the threshold router is
whether high-confidence little predictions are correct on the samples that exit
early. Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-routing} illustrates this relationship between
confidence quality, routing error, exit ratio, and cascade accuracy.

coupled cascade, whereas the independent baseline can produce high exit ratios
without equally strong routing quality or capacity allocation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This section interprets the results in relation to the three research
questions. The discussion focuses on what the experiments show about joint
cascade design, retraining losses, and confidence-based routing, and then
summarizes the main limitations of the study.

\subsection{Joint Cascade Design}

The results support the idea that a big/little cascade should be designed as a
coupled system rather than as two unrelated classifiers. The joint NAS pipeline
selects architectures using cascade accuracy, expected cascade FLOPs, exit
ratio, and deployment constraints at the same time. In contrast, the
independent baseline first optimizes little and big models as standalone
classifiers and only later combines them into cascade pairs. The RQ1 comparison
therefore evaluates an end-to-end cascade-design pipeline. It does not isolate
architecture search from joint retraining, but it does test the full procedure
that would be used to design a deployed cascade.

At the fixed operating threshold $\tau=0.70$, the co-searched cascades achieve
substantially higher cascade accuracy than the independent baseline. The exact
accuracy-compute point depends on the retraining loss: C3 is the cheaper
cascade-aware point, while C4 obtains higher accuracy by deferring more samples
and therefore using more FLOPs. This is the strongest result in the study: the
joint search does not merely find a more accurate but more expensive cascade;
it produces a set of stronger operating points under the same memory envelope.
The independent baseline still exits more often, but its component models are
weaker and its routing decisions produce lower final cascade accuracy.

Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-joint-independent} shows this separation for the
high-accuracy C4 setting. It should be read together with the C1-C4 ablation:
C4 demonstrates the upper-accuracy joint setting, while C3 demonstrates the
cheaper cascade-aware setting. Additional diagnostic plots in
Appendix~\ref{app:diagnostic-plots} break down little, big, and expected
cascade FLOPs, showing how the selected architecture sizes and exit ratios
combine to determine the final compute cost.

The FLOPs breakdown is important because the little model is evaluated for
every input. In the independent baseline, the little model is selected as a
standalone classifier under a parameter budget, so it can still be expensive in
MACs if it uses costly convolutions on high-resolution feature maps. Parameter
count and FLOPs are therefore not interchangeable. The independent little model
is also optimized as if it should classify the full data distribution, while in
a cascade it mainly needs to classify easy, high-confidence inputs and defer
harder cases. The joint search sees this routing behaviour during evaluation:
it directly optimizes expected cascade FLOPs and cascade accuracy, where the
little-model cost is always paid and wrong confident exits are harmful. This
encourages cheaper little branches that act more like cascade front-ends rather
than standalone classifiers. This partly explains why the independent baseline
can have a high exit ratio while still producing a weaker accuracy-FLOPs
trade-off.

\subsection{Effect of Retraining Loss}

The C1-C4 ablation shows that the retraining objective changes how the
selected cascades route inputs, but the architecture selected by NAS remains a
major factor in the final accuracy-FLOPs trade-off. CE remains a strong
baseline on CIFAR-10. The cascade-aware loss without smoothing, C3,
slightly improves cascade accuracy and lowers expected FLOPs compared with C1,
but the difference is small. The clearer effect comes from label smoothing:
C2 and C4 reduce harmful routing errors and obtain higher cascade accuracy by
deferring more samples to the big model. This makes the result useful even
though it is not a simple standalone win for the custom loss: the architecture
selection, operating threshold, confidence behaviour, and smoothing all shape
the final cascade.

Figure~\ref{fig:rq2-ablation} should therefore be read as evidence about
operating behaviour rather than as a clean replacement of one loss by another.
The cascade-aware variants are competitive, and C3 moves in the desired
direction by achieving marginally higher accuracy with slightly lower FLOPs
than C1. However, the difference between C1 and C3 is small compared with the
effect of smoothing in C2 and C4. The current CIFAR-10 setting therefore does
not prove that the cascade-aware terms are the dominant source of improvement,
although the loss remains useful as a routing-aware regularizer and a plausible
direction for further tuning.

\subsection{Routing and Confidence}

The experiments also show that confidence routing is central to cascade
usefulness. The system is not determined only by little-model and big-model
standalone accuracy. It also depends on whether the little model is confident
on the right samples. A high exit ratio is beneficial only when the exited
samples are mostly correct; otherwise, it creates avoidable routing errors
where the cascade exits early even though the big model would have been
correct. This is why ECE, routing error rate, and exit ratio are necessary
alongside accuracy and FLOPs when interpreting the results. In the final
ablation, C4 has the lowest routing error, while C3 has a higher exit ratio and
lower FLOPs; this illustrates the practical tension between compute saving and
safe early exits.

Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-routing} shows why routing cannot be reduced to a single
exit-ratio number. A cascade with more early exits may be cheaper, but it is
only useful if those exits are reliable. Conversely, a cascade with strong
accuracy may still use more computation if it defers many samples. Routing
error captures the specific failure case that matters for the threshold router:
the little model exits confidently and incorrectly on a sample that the big
model would have classified correctly. The appendix compares the independent
baseline's routing diagnostics with the C1-C4 ablations. This supports a
cautious interpretation: co-search does not always produce lower ECE, but the
lower-routing joint variants reduce harmful early exits more effectively.

Figure~\ref{fig:rq3-threshold-sensitivity} shows why the fixed threshold
$\tau=0.70$ should be interpreted as one operating point rather than a universal
best setting. The same trained cascade can move along a spectrum from cheaper
and more exit-heavy to more accurate and more deferral-heavy simply by changing
the threshold. This is important for deployment, because the preferred
threshold depends on whether the target device prioritizes accuracy, latency,
or energy. The threshold should also be interpreted probabilistically. If the
little model were perfectly calibrated, predictions with confidence around
0.70 would still be expected to be wrong about 30\% of the time. A threshold of
0.70 therefore does not guarantee correctness; it defines how much uncertainty
the cascade accepts before deferring to the big model. We use $\tau=0.70$
 because it is low enough to allow the little model to exit on a meaningful
 fraction of inputs under the limited proxy-evaluation budget, but high enough
 to avoid routing
many clearly low-confidence predictions through the little model. Since the
joint search also evaluates candidates at this threshold, the selected
architectures are partly optimized for $\tau=0.70$. This is appropriate for a
fixed-threshold deployment setting, but it also means that the fixed-threshold
results may be relatively favourable to the threshold used during search.

For C3 and C4, $\tau=0.70$ also enters the retraining objective itself. The
cascade-aware loss uses a soft routing probability based on the little model's
confidence relative to the threshold, so it encourages behaviour that is useful
near this operating point. This helps explain the threshold-sweep shape: the
cascade-aware variants, especially C3, are competitive at lower and mid
thresholds where early exits remain common, but can lose their advantage once
the threshold is pushed above $0.70$ and the routing policy moves away from the
one emphasized during training.

\subsection{Pico 2 Deployment Interpretation}

The Pico 2 measurements support the main dynamic-inference assumption behind
the analytical FLOPs metric: early exits are cheaper than deferrals. For the
Arch2 cascades, samples that exit after the little model take about 40 ms,
whereas deferred samples take about 204 ms because the big model is evaluated
after the little model. This approximately five-fold per-sample latency
difference means that the exit ratio has a direct hardware effect. C1 and C3,
which exit on about 43\% of samples, have lower average latency than C2 and C4,
which defer more often. Thus, the measured latency confirms that avoiding the
big model produces the expected compute saving on the Pico 2.

At the same time, the hardware results show why analytical MACs should be
treated as a proxy rather than a complete deployment model. Within the Arch2
family, the average analytical FLOPs increase from roughly 0.87M MACs for C1
and C3 to roughly 1.03M MACs for C4, and measured latency increases in the same
direction. This agreement is useful because it validates the FLOPs objective as
a reasonable ranking signal among closely related cascades. However, the
independent baseline breaks any simple FLOPs-to-latency interpretation. Its
average cascade cost is about 2.05M MACs, but its measured latency is 425.77
ms, far more than a simple doubling of the Arch2 latency would suggest. The
independent little model alone takes about 131.93 ms, more than three times the
Arch2 little path, even though both are small enough to fit the search budget.

This mismatch is likely caused by hardware details that are intentionally
abstracted away during NAS. The analytical FLOPs calculation counts multiply-
accumulate work, but it does not model TensorFlow Lite Micro kernel
implementations, depthwise versus standard convolution efficiency on the Pico
2, memory movement, cache effects, tensor layout conversions, or arena
allocation pressure. It also does not capture the fact that deployment memory
is split between flash-resident model data and SRAM-resident tensor arenas. The
Arch2 models used a stable 128/256 KiB little/big arena split, whereas the
independent baseline required a much larger big-model arena and several
diagnostic builds. This reinforces the distinction made in the methodology:
analytical MACs and estimated memory are useful for search and comparison, but
final deployment still requires hardware measurement.

Overall, the Pico 2 results strengthen the interpretation of RQ1 and RQ3. The
joint Arch2 cascades do not only look better on the analytical
accuracy--FLOPs frontier; they also run substantially faster on the target
microcontroller. The measured route split explains much of the latency
difference within a fixed architecture, while the independent baseline shows
that a high exit ratio is not sufficient when the little model itself is slow
and memory-hungry. The deployment results therefore support the expected
compute savings of threshold-based cascades, but they also show that FLOPs,
exit ratio, and memory must be interpreted together rather than as independent
deployment predictors.

\subsection{Dataset and Framework Interpretation}

The dataset also affects the interpretation. CIFAR-10 is small enough to make
the full NAS and retraining pipeline feasible, but it may not create a strong
division between easy examples for the little model and hard examples for the
big model. In several runs, the little model approaches the big model's
standalone accuracy. This reduces the natural room for a cascade-aware loss to
specialize the two models. A more complex dataset could make the big/little
division of labour clearer, but would also increase the cost of NAS.

Overall, the contributions are the joint NSGA-II co-search setup, the
cascade-aware co-training loss, the C1-C4 retraining ablation, and the analysis
of how threshold choice, exit ratio, routing errors, and capacity allocation
shape the final accuracy-FLOPs trade-off. The results show that this framework
can find memory-feasible dynamic cascades and that joint co-search is more
effective than independently searching the two models at finding strong cascade
operating points. The analysis further exposes why different operating points
trade accuracy against expected compute.

\subsection{Limitations}

The main limitations concern the gap between a feasible NAS experiment and the
ideal exhaustive optimization of a deployed MCU cascade.

\subsubsection{Proxy Training}
The architecture search relies on proxy training to evaluate candidate
cascades. This is necessary because a full retraining run for every little-big
pair would make the search computationally impractical. However, proxy training
also means that each candidate is judged before it has necessarily reached its
full potential. Some architectures may learn slowly and therefore appear weak
during the proxy stage, while others may look strong early but not retain that
advantage after longer training. This limits how confidently the search ranking
can be interpreted. The final retraining step reduces this issue for selected
architectures, but it does not recover candidates that were discarded because
their proxy performance was misleading.

\subsubsection{Dataset Size}
The experiments use CIFAR-10 with a train/validation split. CIFAR-10 is useful
for this project because it is small enough to support repeated NAS,
retraining, threshold evaluation, export, and microcontroller testing. At the
same time, the dataset is limited in both size and visual complexity. The
learned cascade behaviour may therefore depend on
CIFAR-10-specific properties, such as the distribution of easy and difficult
samples, the class set, and the image resolution. A larger or more complex
dataset could change which architectures are feasible, which loss functions are
useful, and how often the little model can safely handle an example without
deferring to the big model.

\subsubsection{Search Space Size}
The search space contains a very large number of possible cascade
configurations. Each cascade is defined not only by one architecture, but by a
little model, a big model, and how their capacities interact under the routing
rule. Even with a restricted CNN design space, the number of possible
combinations grows quickly as block depth, channel count, kernel size,
convolution type, and residual choices are varied. With relatively few hard
bounds, the search must explore a space containing many infeasible or
unpromising candidates before it finds architectures that satisfy the Pico 2
constraints and still perform well. This can make the search inefficient and
increases the chance that good feasible regions are only sparsely sampled. More
role-specific constraints, stronger feasibility filters, or hardware-informed
priors could make the search more focused.

\subsubsection{Search and Training Budget}
The NAS experiments use a finite search and training budget. The joint search uses a
population of 20 for 20 generations, while the independent baseline uses 20
individuals for 10 generations per role. Each candidate is proxy-trained for
only 5 epochs. These settings make the experiment feasible, but they also mean
that the search only samples a small fraction of the possible architecture
space. The independent setup also matches the approximate number of
proxy-trained candidates rather than giving each role the same 20-generation
evolutionary depth as the joint search. This was a practical compromise because
the independent baseline performs two sequential searches, but a stricter
comparison would be to increase the generation budget for both methods.
Increasing the number of generations, population size, or proxy-training epochs
could improve the reliability of the selected architectures, but would also
increase runtime substantially. The same applies to final retraining: 50 epochs
is long enough to obtain useful comparisons across many variants, but many
CIFAR-style training setups use longer schedules such as 100-200 epochs. A
longer retraining schedule could improve absolute accuracy and might give the
cascade-aware loss more time to influence confidence and routing behaviour. The
reported results should therefore be understood as the outcome of a practical
NAS budget rather than an exhaustive optimum.

\subsubsection{Float32 Deployment}
The deployment experiment uses float32 TensorFlow Lite Micro models instead of
quantized models. This choice makes the export path easier to validate because
the deployed models remain close to the PyTorch models used during training. It
also avoids adding quantization error as a separate variable in the first
deployment experiment. However, float32 inference is not the most
memory-efficient representation for an MCU. Compared with int8 quantization,
float32 weights require substantially more memory, reducing the amount of model
capacity available within the same Pico 2 budget. This matters directly for the
project because the search is trying to allocate capacity between the little
and big models. A quantized deployment could allow larger architectures, lower
latency, or different routing behaviour, but it could also change confidence
values and therefore affect the cascade's exit decisions.

\subsubsection{Pico Deployment Measurements}
The Pico 2 evaluation measures on-device latency and routing behaviour, which
is an important step beyond reporting only proxy FLOPs. The route-aware energy
test adds an initial direct measurement of energy on a 1,000-sample embedded
subset, but it is less comprehensive than the 10,000-sample serial latency
evaluation and is reported only for the final joint-vs-independent comparison.
For microcontroller applications, especially battery-powered ones, energy may
be as important as latency. A cascade that is faster is not necessarily the most
energy-efficient if its power draw differs across model paths or if repeated
big model invocations have a disproportionate cost. Future evaluation should
therefore include repeated energy measurements across all C1--C4 variants,
additional boards, and quantized models in addition to accuracy, route split,
and latency.

\subsection{Future Work}
Future work should first evaluate the framework on a more complex dataset such
as CIFAR-100 or Tiny ImageNet. This would test whether the cascade-aware design
becomes more useful when there is a clearer separation between easy and hard
inputs. A stronger dataset would also make it easier to judge whether the big
model provides a meaningful advantage over the little model.

A second direction is to repeat the comparison with a larger search and
training budget. The current setup matches the approximate number of
proxy-trained candidates between joint and independent NAS, but independent NAS
uses 10 generations per role while joint NAS evolves complete pairs for 20
generations. Future experiments should test larger population sizes, generation
counts, and proxy-training budgets for both methods to evaluate whether the
observed advantage remains under a larger search budget. The selected
architectures should also be retrained for longer schedules, such as 100-200
epochs, to estimate final accuracy more precisely and to test whether
cascade-aware losses need more training time to shape confidence and routing
behaviour.

A further direction is to improve the routing mechanism. This work uses maximum
softmax confidence because it is simple, cheap, and directly compatible with an
early-exit cascade. Other routing rules could be tested, including margin-based
routing, entropy-based routing, or a small learned router. These alternatives
may identify uncertain samples more reliably than raw confidence.

The cascade-aware loss should also be explored more systematically. The current
weights were chosen from a small set of manual configuration tests, where the
setting with a higher defer-cost term showed the most potential for C3. This is
not enough to claim that the setting is globally optimal. A broader multi-seed
sweep over defer cost, confident-wrong penalty, routing sharpness, auxiliary
loss weights, and training threshold could show whether the loss can more
consistently improve cascade accuracy or exit ratio. It would also be useful to
compare training at the same threshold used for final evaluation against
training with a range of thresholds.

Finally, future experiments should include stronger deployment-oriented
baselines and measurements. Single little-only and big-only models, cascades
matched by FLOPs, and quantized int8 variants would make the accuracy-compute
trade-off easier to interpret. Hardware evaluation should also include latency,
peak memory, and energy measurements, since FLOPs and parameter count are only
proxies for embedded deployment cost.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This work investigated NAS-driven big/little cascades for input-adaptive
inference under a small deployment-memory budget. The proposed framework
combines joint cascade architecture search, constrained model selection,
retraining ablations, and threshold-based evaluation. The results show that
joint co-search can find substantially better cascades than an independently
searched baseline. At the main threshold $\tau=0.70$, the joint variants cover
a better accuracy-FLOPs region: C3 is the cheaper cascade-aware operating
point, while C4 is the higher-accuracy, higher-compute operating point. Both
support the central argument that the little model, big model, and routing rule
should be designed as a coupled system.

The retraining ablation shows a more nuanced result. The custom cascade-aware
loss is not a dramatic standalone replacement for CE on CIFAR-10, but the C3
setting slightly improves accuracy and lowers FLOPs compared with CE. Combining
cascade-aware loss with label smoothing gives the highest average
accuracy and the lowest routing error among the C1-C4 variants, but it also
uses more FLOPs. This suggests that cascade-aware objectives are useful, but
their benefit depends on how they interact with confidence calibration, exit
ratio, and the selected architecture.

The experiments also show that threshold choice, exit ratio, calibration, and
routing error are essential for interpreting cascade systems. A cascade is only
efficient when the little model exits on samples it can classify correctly; a
high exit ratio alone is not sufficient. The Pico 2 deployment confirms this
hardware view: the joint Arch2 cascade has much lower measured latency and
lower route-aware energy than the independent baseline, even though the
independent baseline exits more often. This shows why analytical MACs are
useful search proxies but cannot fully capture TensorFlow Lite Micro latency,
arena pressure, and board-level energy behaviour. Overall, the project
demonstrates a reproducible framework for NAS-driven dynamic inference and
shows that joint cascade design can improve the accuracy-compute trade-off,
while leaving open important questions about loss design, calibration, larger
datasets, quantization, and hardware-aware optimization.

\label{app:diagnostic-plots}

This appendix collects supporting plots that are useful for interpreting the
main results, but are not necessary for the primary RQ1-RQ3 narrative. The
figures should be read as diagnostic evidence rather than additional headline
results.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{proxy_pareto_frontier_knee_unique_selected.pdf}
\caption{Proxy Pareto frontier used to select joint architectures for full
retraining. The selected candidates are chosen from the compute-saving knee
region after feasibility checks and the constraint
$F_{\mathrm{cascade}} < F_b$, not simply by taking the three highest
proxy-accuracy points. The figure therefore documents the selection heuristic;
final claims are based on fully retrained test-set results rather than the
5-epoch proxy scores.}
\label{fig:appendix-proxy-selection}
\end{figure}

\centering

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{flops_breakdown_ablation_c1_c4_t070.pdf}
\end{minipage}
\caption{FLOPs breakdown at $\tau=0.70$. The independent baseline and joint
variants use different selected architecture pairs, while the C1-C4 joint
ablations use the same selected architectures. Changes in expected cascade
FLOPs within the joint ablation therefore mainly reflect differences in exit
ratio caused by the retraining objective.}
\label{fig:appendix-flops-breakdown}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.62\textwidth]{independent_ece_routing_at_070.pdf}
\caption{Independent-baseline routing diagnostics at $\tau=0.70$. The
independent baseline can have lower ECE than some joint variants, but it still
has lower cascade accuracy, showing that calibration alone is not sufficient to
explain the final trade-off.}
\label{fig:appendix-routing-diagnostics}
\end{figure}
